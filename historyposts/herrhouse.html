Hans Herr House

Just as the Amish today use member’s houses for services, the early Mennonites used the Herr home and barn for their own gatherings. Though for many, this house may look plain and small, complete with simple details and diminutive windows, for the Germans who came over, building in stone was a sign of achievement in status. Those who came to Pennsylvania from Germany and Switzerland were likely living in wood homes before they emigrated.

“Until the Revolution, it was the bishops and preacher class in Lancaster, Montgomery, and Bucks counties that generally built houses of stone similar to the Herr House, particularly where the dwelling was also meant to serve as a community focal point, a place of religious activity. From these houses, which took the places of churches, the religious community was administered and firmly held together”
—William Woys Weaver, The Pennsylvania German House: European Antecedents and New World Forms.

After visiting the Hans Herr House, I suggest heading to Speckled Hen Coffee or Lampeter Cafe before driving around south-western Lancaster County where you’ll find an array of stone and brick homes, some with porch on porch details, as well as numerous tobacco barns which feature distinctive exterior walls with wooden louvers that aerate the hanging plants inside.
